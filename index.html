Mirazul Islam @ Vanderbilt University

Lineage Tracking | Temporal Recording | CRISPR Screen | Clonal Dynamics

Mammalian development is a remarkable progress, comprising a highly orchestrated series of cell divisions and lineage diversifications. I am studing mammalian development and tumoregenesis using newly developed tools and platforms. In addition, I am studing a new type of stem cell population in mouse intestinal epithelium, that is indispensible in regeneration, using cutting edge technologies, including CRISPR barcoding, genomics, CreERT2 mouse model, and organoids culture.

Background


Biography
I graduated from University of Dhaka in 2014 with a B.S. in Biochemistry and Molecular Biology. Then I received my M.S. in Medical Science from University of Malaya in 2016. During this time, I received research training from a few institutes including Academia Sinica, Children Hospital Los Angeles (CHLA), Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory, German Cancer Research Center (DKFZ). After my M.S. degree, I joined Adam Bass’ Lab at the Broad Institute and DFCI to work for The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) project until 2018. I am interested in developmental disorders like cancers. In addition, I am fascinated by the plasticity and the clonal dynamics as well as cells-of-origin of intestinal epithelium. Outside of the lab, I play badminton, cricket, and soccer, and I am passionate about quality science education development.










RESEARCH



Mammalian development

How lineage diversification, cell-type specification, and organ size maintenance happen within a limited time?

1) Single-cell lineage tracking.
2) Single-cell temporal recording.
3) Catalog of cellular turnover.

CRISPR technology

We use single-cell CRISPR platform called NSC-seq to perform screening.

1) Single-cell CRISPR screening using KO plasmid libraries.
2) Isoform sgRNA expression under U6 promoter and off-target effect.

Tumoregenesis

Studying clonal origin of colorectal cancer.

1) Part of the Human Tumor Atlas Network (HTAN).
2) ApcMin/+ mouse model.
3) Organ-specific cancer risk and cell divsion histories as a risk factor.
